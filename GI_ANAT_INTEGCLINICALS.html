🔷 Peritoneum Conditions

Acute Peritonitis

Inflammation of peritoneum from infection or chemical irritation (ruptured appendix, perforated ulcer, trauma)
Parietal peritoneum has somatic innervation → sharp, localized pain. Visceral peritoneum has autonomic innervation → dull, poorly localized pain
Pain 8-10/10, board-like rigidity, rebound tenderness, guarding, fever, nausea/vomiting
Visceral pain (dull, diffuse) comes FIRST, then parietal pain (sharp, localized) when inflammation reaches peritoneum. This explains pain migration in appendicitis!
"Visceral = Vague; Parietal = Precise"
Surgical emergency: Requires source control + antibiotics. Delay → sepsis → death
Peritoneum Emergency

Ascites

Fluid accumulation in peritoneal cavity due to portal hypertension, hypoalbuminemia, or inflammation
Peritoneal cavity normally has minimal lubricating fluid. Massive accumulation elevates diaphragm → limits excursion
Abdominal distension, shifting dullness, fluid wave, dyspnea (elevated diaphragm), early satiety
Ascites is FLUID, not fat. It transilluminates poorly but shows shifting dullness. Don't confuse with obesity!
"Shifting dullness = fluid moves with position change"
Paracentesis needed for diagnosis. Check SAAG (Serum-Ascites Albumin Gradient) to determine cause
Peritoneum Clinical

Volvulus

Twisting of bowel loop around its mesentery → obstruction + vascular compromise → ischemia
Mesentery contains blood vessels. Twist = kinked vessels = ischemia. Sigmoid colon most common (long, mobile mesentery)
Acute abdominal pain, distension, absolute constipation, vomiting. "Coffee bean" sign on X-ray
Volvulus is a SURGICAL EMERGENCY. Time to detorsion = bowel viability. Don't wait for labs!
"Twist = Ischemia = Necrosis = Death if not fixed"
Peritoneum Emergency

Peritoneal Carcinomatosis

Cancer seeding throughout peritoneal cavity
Primary sources: Ovarian cancer (most common), gastric cancer, colorectal cancer, pancreatic cancer
"Omental caking" on imaging, ascites, bowel obstruction, weight loss

🔴 Esophagus & Stomach Conditions

GERD (Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease)

Failure of lower esophageal sphincter (LES) → gastric acid refluxes into esophagus
LES at T10 (esophageal hiatus). Hiatal hernia weakens LES. Esophageal epithelium not designed for acid exposure
Retrosternal burning (heartburn), regurgitation, worse after meals/lying down, chronic cough
GERD can mimic cardiac pain! Always rule out MI first in patients with chest pain, especially if risk factors present
"GERD = Gastric content Escapes, Refluxes, Damages"
Hepatobiliary Clinical

Esophageal Varices

Portal hypertension → blood shunts via portosystemic anastomosis at lower esophagus
Left gastric vein (portal) ↔ Azygos vein (systemic/SVC). Dilated submucosal veins = varices
Often asymptomatic until rupture → massive hematemesis, hypotension, shock
Don't confuse with Mallory-Weiss tear (mucosal tear from vomiting) or peptic ulcer. Varices = dilated veins from portal HTN
"ERUR: Esophagus, Rectum, Umbilicus, Retroperitoneum = sites of portosystemic shunting"
EMERGENCY: Mortality up to 20% per bleed. Needs IV octreotide, endoscopic band ligation, possible TIPS
Hepatobiliary Emergency

Barrett's Esophagus vs. Esophageal Varices

Both affect lower esophagus but different mechanisms
Barrett's: Metaplasia (squamous → columnar) from chronic GERD → ↑ adenocarcinoma risk
Varices: Dilated veins from portal HTN → risk of rupture/hemorrhage
Barrett's = epithelial change (precancerous). Varices = vascular dilation (bleeding risk). Don't mix mechanisms!
"Barrett's = Burned → Barrett's → Cancer; Varices = Veins → Rupture → Bleed"
Hepatobiliary Clinical

Achalasia

Loss of myenteric (Auerbach's) plexus → LES fails to relax → food backs up
Myenteric plexus lies between muscle layers. Vagus nerve damage (Chagas, idiopathic) → plexus degeneration
Dysphagia (solids AND liquids from start), regurgitation of undigested food, chest pain, weight loss
Achalasia affects BOTH solids and liquids from the START (motility disorder). Mechanical obstruction starts with solids only, then progresses
"Bird's beak on barium swallow = Achalasia"
Hepatobiliary Clinical

Peptic Ulcer Disease: Duodenal vs. Gastric

Imbalance: aggressive factors (acid, pepsin) > defensive factors (mucus, prostaglandins)
Duodenal ulcer: First part of duodenum (foregut), near gastroduodenal artery
Gastric ulcer: Lesser curvature, near left gastric artery
Duodenal: Pain RELIEVED by food (buffers acid), hunger pain, night pain
Gastric: Pain WORSENED by food (stimulates acid), weight loss, early satiety
Posterior duodenal ulcer → erodes gastroduodenal artery → MASSIVE hemorrhage (surgical emergency). Anterior ulcer → perforates → peritonitis
"Duodenal = Dinner helps; Gastric = Food aggravates"
Alarm symptoms: weight loss, anemia, dysphagia, vomiting, GI bleed, age >55 → need endoscopy to rule out cancer!
Hepatobiliary Clinical

Gastric Cancer: Lymphatic Spread

Adenocarcinoma from chronic gastritis → atrophy → metaplasia → dysplasia
Stomach lymph → gastric/gastroepiploic nodes → celiac nodes → thoracic duct → left supraclavicular
Often presents late: weight loss, early satiety, anemia, epigastric pain. May have Virchow's node or Sister Mary Joseph nodule
Gastric cancer often SILENT early! Any change in bowel habits + anemia in >50 y/o = colonoscopy. Don't dismiss vague symptoms
"Virchow's node = Left supraclavicular = Gastric CA metastasis via thoracic duct"
Hepatobiliary Oncology

🟤 Liver & Gallbladder Conditions

Acute Cholecystitis

Cystic duct obstruction (gallstone) → bile stasis → inflammation ± infection
Gallbladder in fossa between liver segments IV & V. Inflammation irritates parietal peritoneum → localized RUQ pain
RUQ pain (constant, not colicky), fever, nausea/vomiting, positive Murphy's sign (inspiratory arrest during RUQ palpation)
Gallbladder pain referral: Visceral pain (epigastric/RUQ, T7-T9) → parietal pain (localized RUQ) → referred pain (right scapula via phrenic C3-C5)
"Murphy's sign = inspiratory arrest during RUQ palpation = cholecystitis"
Complications: gangrene, perforation, empyema, gallstone ileus. Needs urgent cholecystectomy
Hepatobiliary Emergency

Cystic Artery Variations: Surgical Trap

Cystic artery usually from right hepatic artery (75%), but 25-50% have variations: hepatic artery proper, gastroduodenal, left hepatic, multiple arteries
During cholecystectomy, misidentifying cystic artery → bleeding or bile duct injury. Calot's triangle must be cleared before clipping
"Cystic artery always from right hepatic" → FALSE. 25-50% have variations. Always confirm anatomy before clipping!
"Calot's triangle: Cystic duct + Common hepatic duct + Liver edge = Cystic artery inside"
Bile duct injury = catastrophic complication. "Critical view of safety" required before clipping any structure
Hepatobiliary Surgical

Budd-Chiari Syndrome vs. Portal Hypertension

Budd-Chiari: Hepatic vein thrombosis → hepatic outflow obstruction
Portal HTN: Portal vein obstruction → portal inflow problem
Budd-Chiari: Hepatic veins drain liver → IVC
Portal HTN: Portal vein brings GI blood → liver
Budd-Chiari: Painful hepatomegaly, ascites, acute liver failure
Portal HTN: Ascites, varices, splenomegaly, caput medusae
Both cause ascites → but different mechanisms! Budd-Chiari = hepatic VEIN thrombosis (hypercoagulable states). Portal HTN = liver cirrhosis
"Budd-Chiari = Backed-up liver veins; Portal HTN = Pressured portal vein"
Hepatobiliary Clinical

🟢 Pancreas Conditions

Acute Pancreatitis: Why Back Pain?

Premature activation of pancreatic enzymes → autodigestion → inflammation
Pancreas is retroperitoneal (behind peritoneum). Inflammation irritates posterior abdominal wall → pain refers to back (T10-L1)
Severe epigastric pain RADIATING to back, nausea/vomiting, worse after eating. Amylase/lipase >3x upper limit
Back pain is KEY! Pancreas is retroperitoneal → inflammation irritates posterior abdominal wall → pain refers to back. Don't miss this!
"SAD PUCKER = Retroperitoneal organs (Suprarenals, Aorta/IVC, Duodenum 2-4, Pancreas except tail, Ureters, Colon ascending/descending, Kidneys, Esophagus, Rectum)"
Severe signs: Cullen's sign (periumbilical ecchymosis), Grey-Turner sign (flank ecchymosis) = hemorrhagic pancreatitis
Hepatobiliary Emergency

Whipple Procedure: Why Remove Duodenum?

Pancreatic head cancer → resection needed
Blood supply to duodenum (gastroduodenal + inferior pancreaticoduodenal arteries) passes THROUGH pancreatic head. Remove head → devascularize duodenum → must remove both
"Duodenum has independent blood supply" → FALSE. 2nd part has dual supply (celiac + SMA), but both run through pancreatic head. Can't preserve duodenum if head is removed
"Pancreatic head = duodenal blood highway" → block the highway → duodenum dies
Hepatobiliary Surgical

🔵 Small Intestine Conditions

Small Bowel Obstruction (SBO)

Mechanical blockage of small intestine
Adhesions (most common - post-surgical), hernias, malignancy, Crohn's disease, volvulus
Colicky periumbilical pain (midgut T10), vomiting (bilious if proximal), distension, absolute constipation. High-pitched "tinkling" bowel sounds early
Pain pattern: Periumbilical (visceral, midgut T10) → localized if parietal peritoneum involved. Bilious vomiting = obstruction distal to ampulla of Vater
"SBO = Small = Soon vomiting, Small distension, Soon pain"
Strangulation signs: Constant pain (not colicky), fever, tachycardia, peritonitis → SURGICAL EMERGENCY
Peritoneum Emergency

Meckel's Diverticulum: Rule of 2s

Remnant of vitelline (omphalomesenteric) duct
Located in ILEUM (midgut), 2 feet from ileocecal valve. May contain ectopic gastric/pancreatic tissue
Rule of 2s: 2% of population, 2 feet from ileocecal valve, 2 inches long, presents before age 2, 2 types of ectopic tissue
Meckel's is in the ILEUM (midgut), NOT colon. It's the MOST COMMON congenital GI anomaly. Technetium-99m pertechnetate scan detects gastric mucosa
"Meckel's = Midgut, 2 feet from ICV, 2 tissues (gastric/pancreatic), bleeds from acid"
Peritoneum Congenital

Crohn's Disease vs. Ulcerative Colitis

Crohn's: Transmural inflammation from immune dysregulation
UC: Mucosal inflammation limited to colon
Crohn's: Can affect ANY part of GI tract (mouth to anus), terminal ileum most common, "skip lesions"
UC: Continuous inflammation, colon only, no granulomas
Crohn's: Fistulas, strictures, abscesses, malabsorption (B12 deficiency if terminal ileum)
UC: Bloody diarrhea, urgency, tenesmus, ↑ colon cancer risk
Crohn's vs UC: Crohn's = transmural, skip lesions, any location, granulomas, fistulas. UC = mucosal only, continuous, colon only, no granulomas, no fistulas
"Crohn's = Can Reach Anywhere; UC = Only Colon"

🟣 Large Intestine Conditions

Acute Appendicitis: Pain Migration Explained

Luminal obstruction (fecalith, lymphoid hyperplasia) → increased pressure → ischemia → bacterial overgrowth → inflammation
Phase 1 (Visceral pain): Appendix inflammation → midgut visceral afferents (T10) → periumbilical pain
Phase 2 (Somatic pain): Inflammation reaches parietal peritoneum → somatic nerves (T12-L1) → localized RLQ pain at McBurney's point
Classic migration: periumbilical pain (visceral) → RLQ pain at McBurney's point (parietal). Fever, nausea, anorexia
Appendicitis is one of FEW diagnoses made by history + physical alone! BUT presentation can be atypical in: elderly, pregnant women, children, immunocompromised
"McBurney's point = 2/3 from umbilicus to ASIS = classic appendicitis tenderness"
Perforation: Sudden pain relief → diffuse peritonitis → sepsis. Time is critical!
Peritoneum Emergency

Diverticulosis vs. Diverticulitis

Diverticulosis: Mucosal herniation through muscularis propria at weak points (vasa recta penetration)
Diverticulitis: Inflamed/infected diverticula
Most common site: Sigmoid colon (highest intraluminal pressure)
Diverticulosis: Asymptomatic outpouchings, may cause painless hematochezia
Diverticulitis: LLQ pain, fever, leukocytosis, change in bowel habits
Diverticulosis (asymptomatic) ≠ Diverticulitis (inflamed). Diverticulosis can cause painless hematochezia. Diverticulitis causes painful LLQ symptoms
"Diverticulosis = Dimples; Diverticulitis = Inflamed Dimples = Pain"
Peritoneum Clinical

Colorectal Cancer: Right vs. Left-Sided

Adenoma → carcinoma sequence (APC → KRAS → p53 mutations)
Right-sided (cecum/ascending): Exophytic growth, wider lumen, liquid contents → late presentation
Left-sided (descending/sigmoid): Annular/"napkin ring" growth, narrower lumen, solid contents → early obstruction
Right: Anemia, vague pain, weight loss, late presentation
Left: Change in bowel habits, hematochezia, obstruction, early symptoms
Colorectal cancer often SILENT early! Screening colonoscopy saves lives. Any change in bowel habits + anemia in >50 y/o = colonoscopy!
"Right = RBC loss (anemia); Left = Lumen narrowing (obstruction)"
Metastasis: Liver (portal drainage), lungs, peritoneum. CEA for monitoring
Peritoneum Oncology

⚫ Rectum & Anus Conditions

Hemorrhoids: Internal vs. External

Dilated venous plexus from increased pressure (constipation, pregnancy, portal HTN)
Internal (above pectinate line): Visceral innervation (painless), portal drainage, internal iliac nodes
External (below pectinate line): Somatic innervation (painful), systemic drainage, inguinal nodes
Internal: Painless bleeding, prolapse
External: Painful, thrombosis, itching
Pectinate line is KEY! Above = visceral innervation (painless), portal drainage, internal iliac nodes. Below = somatic innervation (painful), systemic drainage, inguinal nodes
"Internal = Invisible pain; External = Excruciating"
Peritoneum Clinical

Anal Fissure

Tear in anoderm (below pectinate line) from trauma (hard stool, childbirth)
Location: Posterior midline (90%), anterior midline (10%), lateral = think Crohn's/TB/malignancy
Classic triad: Severe pain with defecation, bright red bleeding, constipation (vicious cycle)
Fissure = EXCRUCIATING pain (somatic innervation below pectinate line). Hemorrhoids = painless (internal) or painful only if thrombosed (external)
"Fissure = Feels like fire with every BM"
Peritoneum Clinical

🔺 Hernias

Indirect vs. Direct Inguinal Hernia

Indirect: Persistent processus vaginalis (congenital)
Direct: Acquired weakness of posterior inguinal canal wall (Hesselbach's triangle)
Indirect: LATERAL to inferior epigastric artery, through deep inguinal ring, follows spermatic cord
Direct: MEDIAL to inferior epigastric artery, through Hesselbach's triangle, does NOT follow testicular descent
Indirect: More common, M>F (86% male), more common on RIGHT, children/young adults
Direct: Older men with weak abdominal muscles, rare in women
Clinical test: Reduce hernia, press deep inguinal ring, patient coughs: NO bulge = indirect. Bulge = direct
"Indirect = In the canal (follows cord); Direct = Directly through wall (Hesselbach's)"
Anterior Surgical

Femoral Hernia: High Strangulation Risk

Herniation through femoral canal (below inguinal ligament)
Below inguinal ligament, through femoral ring, medial to femoral vein. Bounded by femoral vein laterally, lacunar ligament medially
More common in WOMEN (wider pelvis), but overall less common than inguinal. Presents as mass below and lateral to pubic tubercle
Location: BELOW and LATERAL to pubic tubercle (inguinal hernia = above and medial). Femoral hernias have HIGHEST strangulation rate due to sharp, rigid boundaries of femoral ring
"Femoral = Female, Below, Bad (high strangulation risk)"
Strangulation risk: Sharp, rigid boundaries of femoral ring (especially lacunar ligament) → vascular compromise → necrosis
Anterior Emergency

⚫ Posterior Abdominal Wall Conditions

Abdominal Aortic Aneurysm (AAA)

Atherosclerotic weakening → dilation (>3 cm)
Aorta enters at T12, bifurcates at L4. Retroperitoneal, anterior to vertebral column
Often asymptomatic until rupture. Classic triad (ruptured): pulsatile abdominal mass, back/flank pain, hypotension
AAA bifurcates at L4 ("Four"). Palpate at umbilicus level. Screening ultrasound for men 65-75 who ever smoked
"AAA = Aorta At L4, Aneurysm At risk"
Rupture = 100% MORTALITY without surgery! Back pain because retroperitoneal, anterior to vertebral column
Posterior Emergency

Psoas Abscess: Why Thigh Pain?

Infection tracking along psoas sheath (vertebral TB, Crohn's, appendicitis, hematogenous)
Psoas major originates on lumbar vertebrae, inserts on lesser trochanter. Genitofemoral nerve (L1-L2) runs on psoas surface
Fever, back pain, pain radiating to thigh/groin (genitofemoral nerve), limp, positive psoas sign (pain on hip extension)
Psoas sign = pain on passive hip extension. Suggests retrocecal appendicitis OR psoas abscess. Don't assume appendicitis only!
"Psoas = Pain on extension = Pus or Appendix"
Posterior Clinical

Meralgia Paresthetica: Nerve Compression

Compression of lateral femoral cutaneous nerve (L2-L3) under inguinal ligament near ASIS
Purely SENSORY nerve to lateral thigh. Passes under inguinal ligament near ASIS
Burning, tingling, numbness over lateral thigh, NO motor deficit. Risk factors: tight belts, obesity, pregnancy, tool belts
Lateral femoral cutaneous nerve is PURELY SENSORY → no motor weakness! Don't confuse with femoral nerve injury (motor to quadriceps)
"Meralgia = Medial belt → Lateral thigh numbness"
Posterior Clinical

👶 Congenital Anomalies

Omphalocele vs. Gastroschisis

Omphalocele: Failure of midgut to return to abdominal cavity after physiologic herniation
Gastroschisis: Abdominal wall defect (usually right of umbilicus)
Omphalocele: Bowel herniation through umbilicus COVERED by membrane (peritoneum + amnion)
Gastroschisis: Exposed bowel WITHOUT covering sac, paraumbilical location
Omphalocele: Associated with cardiac defects, chromosomal abnormalities (Trisomy 13, 18, 21)
Gastroschisis: NOT associated with chromosomal abnormalities. Younger mothers. Bowel damaged by amniotic fluid exposure
Omphalocele = COVERED by sac. Gastroschisis = NO sac (direct exposure). Don't confuse!
"Omphalocele = Outside but Covered; Gastroschisis = Gut Showing, No Sac"
Anterior Congenital

Hirschsprung's Disease

Failure of neural crest cell migration → absence of enteric ganglia in distal colon
Aganglionic segment → tonic contraction → functional obstruction. Rectosigmoid most common
Newborn failure to pass meconium >48h, abdominal distension, bilious vomiting. Can present later with chronic constipation
Hirschsprung's = NEURAL CREST defect (NOT abdominal wall). Rectal biopsy showing absence of ganglion cells = diagnostic
"Hirschsprung's = No ganglia = No go (obstruction)"
Anterior Congenital

🔺 Portal Hypertension Complications

Portosystemic Anastomoses (ERUR)

Portal HTN → blood shunts to systemic veins → varices
Esophagus: Left gastric vein (portal) ↔ Azygos vein (systemic)
Rectum: Superior rectal (portal) ↔ Middle/inferior rectal (systemic)
Umbilicus: Paraumbilical veins (portal) ↔ Superficial epigastric (systemic)
Retroperitoneum: Veins of Retzius (portal) ↔ Lumbar/renal veins (systemic)
Esophageal varices (hematemesis), hemorrhoids, caput medusae, retroperitoneal varices
Esophageal varices = most dangerous (massive bleed, 20% mortality). Don't miss this in cirrhotic patient with hematemesis!
"ERUR: Esophagus, Rectum, Umbilicus, Retroperitoneum = 'When portal pressure's high, these veins will lie!'"
Esophageal variceal bleed = EMERGENCY. Needs IV octreotide, endoscopic band ligation, possible TIPS

🎯 MASTER CLINICAL PATTERNS TO RECOGNIZE

Pain Migration Patterns:
  • Appendicitis: Periumbilical (visceral T10) → RLQ (parietal)
  • Cholecystitis: Epigastric (visceral T7-T9) → RUQ (parietal) → Right scapula (phrenic C3-C5)
  • Pancreatitis: Epigastric → Back (retroperitoneal T10-L1)
Bleeding Patterns:
  • Hematemesis: Esophageal varices (portal HTN), PUD, Mallory-Weiss
  • Hematochezia: Hemorrhoids, diverticulosis, colorectal CA (left-sided), IBD
  • Melena: Upper GI bleed (PUD, varices, gastritis)
Obstruction Patterns:
  • SBO: Early bilious vomiting, colicky periumbilical pain, distension
  • LBO: Late feculent vomiting, hypogastric pain, prominent distension, obstipation
Portal Hypertension Signs:
  • Varices: Esophageal (hematemesis), rectal (hemorrhoids)
  • Caput medusae: Dilated periumbilical veins
  • Ascites: Shifting dullness, fluid wave
  • Splenomegaly: Hypersplenism
